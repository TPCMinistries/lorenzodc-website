Chapter 2: God and Delegated Authority

Power vs Authority

Power

The ability to act

• Physical strength or force

• Resources, money, influence

• Charisma, persuasion

• Can be seized, earned, or developed

• Based on personal capacity

What you CAN do

Authority

The right to act

• Delegated position from higher source

• Divine or official assignment

• Operates through proper order

• Must be given, received, or submitted to

• Based on alignment with source

What you MAY do

A small police officer may have less physical power than you, but when they raise their hand to stop traffic, everyone obeys—not because of their personal strength, but because they carry delegated authority from the state. Their right to act comes from their position, not their power.

Kingdom leaders function the same way. Jesus had both power (miracles) and authority (divine alignment). The centurion understood that authority flows through proper submission to the chain of command, not through personal might.

Chapter 2: Spiritual Protocol Defined

You Are Kingdom Ambassadors

"Now then we are ambassadors for Christ, as though God did beseech you by us: we pray you in Christ's stead, be ye reconciled to God."
2 Corinthians 5:20 (KJV)

What is Spiritual Protocol?

Spiritual protocol is the divine order of authority and submission that governs how Kingdom ambassadors function under Heaven's governmental structure.

Just as earthly ambassadors represent their nation under strict diplomatic protocol—speaking for their president or prime minister, operating within defined boundaries, answerable to their sending government—we represent Heaven under God's governmental order.

As leaders carrying ambassadorial authority, you have:
  • The authority of the One who sent you (not your own)
  • Responsibility to operate within proper Kingdom order
  • Accountability to those God has placed over you
  • Access to Heaven's resources when properly aligned
  • The weight of representing God's government, not just His gifts
"As we begin to learn more about spiritual protocol, about functioning under another's authority, we find that our ministries are strengthened. Prophetic words will become stronger, teachings will flow with more anointing, and ministries will mature."
— Spiritual Protocol
Understanding Resistance

Why This Teaching Feels Threatening

The Amygdala Hijack

When you hear "submit to authority," your brain's alarm system (the amygdala) may trigger an automatic threat response. This is normal and predictable. Submission feels dangerous to our natural mind because it bypasses our survival instinct for self-protection and control.

What happens in your brain when challenged about authority:
  • Amygdala detects perceived threat: Loss of control, vulnerability, potential for harm
  • Fight/flight/freeze response activates: Body prepares to resist or escape
  • Prefrontal cortex goes offline: Rational thinking shuts down temporarily
  • Emotional reactions dominate: Resistance, defensiveness, anger, or withdrawal
  • Justification mechanisms engage: Mind finds reasons why this doesn't apply to you
"This teaching isn't intuitive to our natural mind. Your brain will fight it because submission challenges the kingdom of self. That resistance you're feeling? It's actually proof you're hearing something that disrupts your flesh's demand for autonomy. The natural man does not receive the things of the Spirit of God."
Expect to feel uncomfortable. That's the signal that you're entering Kingdom territory.
Personal Application: The Brake Hose Story

Expansion Requires Maintenance

After ten days ministering in South Africa—teaching at leadership panels, establishing ministry partnerships, flowing in prophetic moments—I came home spiritually full but physically exhausted. The assignment had been powerful. Lives were changed. Connections were made.

But the next assignment came immediately: teach this very message on delegated authority.

This week, while driving to prepare, a road sign got trapped under my car and pierced my brake hose. The car could still move forward—the engine ran fine, everything seemed functional. But I couldn't stop safely. The pressure was leaking.

And the Spirit whispered: "This is exactly what happens when expansion outruns maintenance. When ministry growth exceeds relationship care."

We can minister globally yet ignore leaks locally:
  • Unresolved tension with those in authority over us
  • Broken relationships we've been too busy to repair
  • Offense we've justified rather than releasing
  • Exhaustion we've spiritualized instead of addressing
  • Critical spirits we've disguised as discernment
The hose—that flexible line of relationship and submission—must stay intact for God's pressure to reach its destination. You can't flow what you can't contain. You can't steward authority if your lines of submission are compromised.

Luke 7:2-10 — The Centurion's Faith

The Centurion's Secret

"A certain centurion's servant, who was dear unto him, was sick, and ready to die. And when he heard of Jesus, he sent unto him the elders of the Jews, beseeching him that he would come and heal his servant... The centurion sent friends to him, saying unto him, Lord, trouble not thyself: for I am not worthy that thou shouldest enter under my roof... For I also am a man set under authority, having under me soldiers, and I say unto one, Go, and he goeth; and to another, Come, and he cometh: and to my servant, Do this, and he doeth it. Luke 7:2-10 (KJV)

What the Centurion Understood:

This was a man of great faith AND a man who had authority AND who was under authority. These three realities were not separate—they were one integrated system.

He recognized that Jesus' authority over sickness didn't come from personal power alone, but from perfect alignment with the Father. He understood the Kingdom operates on principles of submission and delegated authority.

His faith wasn't just faith in Jesus' ability (power). It was faith in Jesus' divine authorization (authority). He knew that words spoken from proper position carry the backing of the one who gave the position.

The Principle of Inseparability:

Let us examine more deeply this principle that it is not a person who is sacred, but rather it is that office in which he stands. We saw this previously in the example of King Saul of Israel.

Who put Saul in his place of authority? Did the people make him king? No, the Lord set him in place as the King of Israel. As wicked as he was, the people did not oust him from his office.

Several times it is recorded that David could have taken his life, but he did not touch "the Lord's anointed." Even though Saul himself had committed evil, because he was the office as king, he could not be touched. The people waited for the Lord to remove him. It was not the person (Saul) who commanded respect; it was the office, the position of authority, delegated by God.

"Whosoever therefore resisteth the power, resisteth the ordinance of God: and they that resist shall receive to themselves damnation."
Romans 13:2 (KJV)
When we find ourselves resisting "the power," those placed in authority over us, we are faced with the fact that we are in reality resisting God Himself. How is this so? A human government can be used by God to execute His plans. As we noted previously, Romans 13:1 tells us "...there is no power but of God: the powers that be are ordained of God."

The Critical Distinction

Submission

Heart attitude toward authority

• Always required of believers

• Internal posture of honor

• Respects the office regardless of person

• Can disagree yet stay submitted

• Maintains spiritual covering

ABSOLUTE

Obedience

Outward action toward directives

• Context-dependent

• External behavior and compliance

• Limited by conscience and Scripture

• "We must obey God rather than men"

• Sometimes requires godly resistance

RELATIVE
"God still requires submission. Submission is absolute, but obedience is relative. Submission is an attitude of the heart, while obedience is an outward action. If an authority tells me to do something that violates my conscience, I can be submissive yet not obedient. Why? Because my heart attitude toward that person might be submissive, even though I cannot perform the outward action of obedience. I can still respect that person, I can still heed him and learn from him; but in this one circumstance I cannot obey him."
Spiritual Protocol, Chapter 2

Examples in Scripture:

Daniel: Submitted to Babylonian authority, served excellently, honored the king—but when commanded to stop praying, he obeyed God rather than men. His submission was absolute (respectful attitude), but his obedience was relative (limited by conscience).

Peter and John: "Whether it be right in the sight of God to hearken unto you more than unto God, judge ye" (Acts 4:19). They maintained a submissive posture toward the Sanhedrin (didn't insult or rebel) while refusing to obey the command to stop preaching.

Authority Exposes What's Already There

"For rulers are not a terror to good works, but to the evil. Wilt thou then not be afraid of the power? do that which is good, and thou shalt have praise of the same: For he is the minister of God to thee for good. But if thou do that which is evil, be afraid; for he beareth not the sword in vain: for he is the minister of God, a revenger to execute wrath upon him that doeth evil."
Romans 13:3-4 (KJV)
"An illustration might be helpful. If you are driving down a highway and you notice a police car parked at the side of the road, how do you react? Some people simply keep driving. But many others automatically ease up on the gas and begin to brake. Why? Because rebellion was already there in their hearts. The Bible tells us that rulers are a terror to the evil because they expose the heart."
— Spiritual Protocol

The Heart Test:

If you are at work and the boss walks in and you then become nervous and suddenly work harder, there is evil in your heart. If you're out somewhere and it makes you anxious to see your pastor coming your way, you have evil in your heart.

But if you are the same regardless of who is around, if you work at the same pace whether your supervisor is present or not, that which you were doing is good, for rulers are not a terror to good works, but to evil.

Rulers are tools of the Lord to reveal to us the evil that is in our hearts.
Authority doesn't create rebellion—it exposes rebellion that was already present.

The Kingdom Pyramid Is Upside Down

"God does not make leaders; He makes servants who become leaders. In the world, the leaders are at the top of a pyramid. There are many at the bottom of the pyramid who labor for those at the top; the ones at the top command respect, obedience and honor. Others serve them. But in the Church, things are just not like that. The Lord takes that pyramid, that organizational structure that man has built and He says, 'What an interesting picture of greed and hatred! I think I like it better this way!'—and He turns it upside down! In the Church, the leaders are the foundation—the bottom of the pyramid."
— Spiritual Protocol
"Whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all. For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many."
Mark 10:43-45 (NIV)
Rather than being served, they serve. Rather than accruing honor, they honor and bless others.
The Church is built upon this foundation.

Leaders don't stand on people to elevate themselves. Leaders position themselves at the foundation and carry the weight so that others can stand. This is why Jesus washed feet. This is why Paul called himself the chief of sinners. This is why true apostolic authority is marked by suffering and sacrifice, not luxury and acclaim.

Exodus 21:1-6 — The Bondservant

Two Types of Servants

"God does not make leaders; He makes servants who become leaders. In the world, the leaders are at the top of a pyramid... But in the Church, the Lord takes that pyramid and says, 'I think I like it better this way!'—and He turns it upside down!"
— Spiritual Protocol

Diakonos

Servant by Choice

Servant in relation to work—does the job well

Doulos

Bondservant

Servant in relation to master—serves the person, not just the task

The Difference

Heart vs Skill

Diakonos refuses the covenant; Doulos embraces it

"In Old Testament times, it was customary to bore a hole through the ear of a bond servant. This was one who was given an opportunity to leave the master, but who decided to stay and serve him. (See Exodus 21:1-6.) A diakonos will serve, but when it is time to bore a hole in his ear, he will refuse. He does not love his leaders enough to become their bond servants. When his time for serving is ended, he is gone."
Spiritual Protocol

We are called to be the bond servants of God. He will bore a hole through our ears in the House of God concerning His purposes as we declare that we love the Lord and that which He has called us to do. We will be His servants forever, marked by our serving and identified with our Master.

Are you a diakonos who serves until it costs too much?
Or a doulos who has said, "Pierce my ear—I'm staying"?

Wait for God's Timing

"The Lord forbid that I should do such a thing to my master, the Lord's anointed, or lay my hand on him; for he is the anointed of the Lord."
1 Samuel 24:6 (NIV)

David's Example:

David was anointed as king while Saul still sat on the throne. God had clearly chosen David as the next king. Saul was persecuting David unjustly, trying to kill him out of jealousy and paranoia.

Twice David had the perfect opportunity to take Saul's life—once in a cave, once while Saul slept. His men urged him: "This is the day the Lord spoke of!" But David refused.

Why? Because it was not David's place to remove what God had put in place. David honored Saul's office, not Saul's behavior. He waited for God's timing, even though waiting was costly and dangerous.

"Sometimes we limit God. We do not acknowledge that He is in control; that He is on the throne. Could it be that what we think needs to come down could still be God's choice in a situation? Perhaps He desires that person to remain. David sinned while he was king of Israel. Was he removed immediately? No; the Lord dealt with him while he was still in office. He was not excluded from reigning; God simply judged him while he was king, thus giving the people a pattern and example of repentance and restoration."
— Spiritual Protocol
If someone is selected and elected by God to fill a certain position, and if He has placed him there, can we not assume that God is big enough to remove him if He so desires?

When we look into the Spirit, we see that there will be times when God must reject certain ones from before Him, much as was the case with Saul. Yet, even as Saul was respected because of his office, so will these be. And if we move in the flesh to take men out of their offices, we will see the hand of God's judgment. The message will be, "Although I reject them, it does not mean I call you to reject them."

Declarations & Action

This Week: Practical Action Steps

Theology without application is information, not transformation. Here's what to do this week:

Step 1: Identify Your Leak

Before the week ends, identify one specific area where you're losing spiritual pressure. Name it. Write it down. Is it unforgiveness toward someone in authority? A critical spirit? Isolation from accountability? Unresolved conflict?

Step 2: Repair One Relationship

Choose one relationship that needs maintenance. This could be with someone over you, beside you, or under you. Initiate reconciliation, apologize where needed, or simply check in. Don't let another week pass with the leak unaddressed.

Step 3: Honor One Person You Disagree With

Find someone in authority over you with whom you disagree. Deliberately honor them this week—not because you agree with their decisions, but because you honor the office God has given them. Speak well of them. Pray for them. Serve them.

"Do not merely listen to the word, and so deceive yourselves. Do what it says."
James 1:22 (NIV)
These three steps will test whether you're a diakonos or a doulos.
